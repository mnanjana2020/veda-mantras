ऋग्वेद
Rig Veda

ॐ

The Rig Veda is the oldest of the four Vedas and one of the oldest extant texts in any Indo-European language. Composed in Vedic Sanskrit between 1500-1200 BCE, it contains 1,028 hymns (suktas) organized into 10 books (mandalas).

These hymns are dedicated to various deities including Agni (fire), Indra (king of gods), Varuna (cosmic order), Surya (sun), and many others. The Rig Veda represents humanity's earliest attempts to understand the cosmos, the divine, and our place in the universe.

"Truth is one; the wise call it by many names."
— Rig Veda 1.164.46

गायत्री मन्त्र / Gayatri Mantra

Source: Rig Veda, Mandala 3, Sukta 62, Verse 10

The most sacred mantra of the Vedas, a prayer to the divine light of consciousness

Mantra Text

ಕನ್ನಡ

ಓಂ ಭೂರ್ಭುವಃ ಸ್ವಃ
ತತ್ಸವಿತುರ್ವರೇಣ್ಯಂ
ಭರ್ಗೋ ದೇವಸ್ಯ ಧೀಮಹಿ
ಧಿಯೋ ಯೋ ನಃ ಪ್ರಚೋದಯಾತ್ ॥

देवनागरी

ॐ भूर्भुवः स्वः
तत्सवितुर्वरेण्यं
भर्गो देवस्य धीमहि
धियो यो नः प्रचोदयात् ॥

IAST Transliteration

Oṃ bhūr bhuvaḥ svaḥ
Tat savitur vareṇyaṃ
Bhargo devasya dhīmahi
Dhiyo yo naḥ pracodayāt

Translations

English Translation

Om. We meditate upon the most excellent radiance of the divine Sun (Savitr), the source of the three worlds — Earth, Atmosphere, and Heaven. May that supreme divine light illuminate our intellects and inspire our understanding.

ಕನ್ನಡ ಅನುವಾದ

ಓಂ. ಭೂಮಿ, ಅಂತರಿಕ್ಷ ಮತ್ತು ಸ್ವರ್ಗಲೋಕಗಳಲ್ಲಿ ವ್ಯಾಪಿಸಿರುವ ಆ ಸವಿತೃ ದೇವನ ಶ್ರೇಷ್ಠವಾದ ತೇಜಸ್ಸನ್ನು ನಾವು ಧ್ಯಾನಿಸುತ್ತೇವೆ. ಆ ದೈವಿಕ ಬೆಳಕು ನಮ್ಮ ಬುದ್ಧಿಯನ್ನು ಪ್ರೇರೇಪಿಸಲಿ ಮತ್ತು ಸನ್ಮಾರ್ಗದಲ್ಲಿ ನಡೆಸಲಿ.

Meaning

Simple Meaning

This is a prayer to the divine light of the Sun, asking for wisdom and clarity of mind. The devotee meditates on the brilliant light that pervades all three worlds (physical, mental, and spiritual) and asks for intellectual and spiritual guidance to walk the path of righteousness.

Deep Meaning

The Gayatri represents the journey from ignorance to enlightenment. Savitr is not merely the physical sun but the inner light of consciousness (Brahman) that illumines all existence. The three vyahrtis (bhūr, bhuvaḥ, svaḥ) represent the gross, subtle, and causal bodies, indicating that this light pervades all levels of existence. "Dhīmahi" suggests deep meditation, not mere intellectual understanding. The prayer is for the awakening of buddhi (discriminative wisdom) that allows one to perceive the Real beyond appearances. This mantra encapsulates the entire Vedantic teaching: that the individual consciousness (Atman) is ultimately one with the cosmic consciousness (Brahman), and through meditation on divine light, this truth is revealed.

Commentary

The Gayatri Mantra is considered the essence of all Vedic wisdom. Traditionally, it was revealed to Sage Vishwamitra and is chanted during the Sandhya Vandana (twilight prayers) at sunrise, noon, and sunset. The Chandogya Upanishad states that just as all leaves are held together by a stalk, all speech (Vedas) is held together by the Gayatri.

The mantra operates on three levels corresponding to the three vyahrtis: Bhūr (Earth/Physical) — Represents the physical world and body; Bhuvaḥ (Atmosphere/Mental) — Represents the mental realm and pranic energy; Svaḥ (Heaven/Spiritual) — Represents the spiritual realm and higher consciousness.

In the Bhagavad Gita, Lord Krishna declares: "Among mantras, I am the Gayatri" (10.35), highlighting its supreme spiritual significance. Regular practice is said to purify the mind, develop concentration, and ultimately lead to Self-realization.

Hymn to Agni

Rig Veda 1.1.1 — The very first verse of the Rig Veda

अग्निमीळे पुरोहितं
यज्ञस्य देवमृत्विजम् ।
होतारं रत्नधातमम् ॥
Transliteration:
Agnim īḷe purohitaṃ
Yajñasya devam ṛtvijam
Hotāraṃ ratnadhātamam

Word-by-Word:
अग्निम् (Agnim) — Agni, the fire god (accusative)
ईळे (Īḷe) — I praise, I worship (1st person singular)
पुरोहितम् (Purohitam) — the one placed in front, the priest (accusative)
यज्ञस्य (Yajñasya) — of the sacrifice (genitive)
देवम् (Devam) — the god, the divine one (accusative)
ऋत्विजम् (Ṛtvijam) — the seasonal priest, ritual officiator (accusative)
होतारम् (Hotāram) — the invoker, the caller of gods (accusative)
रत्नधातमम् (Ratnadhātamam) — the best bestower of treasures (superlative)

Translation:
I praise Agni, the chosen priest,
God of the sacrifice, minister of seasons,
The invoker, best bestower of treasures.

Commentary

This opening verse establishes Agni (Fire) as the central deity of Vedic ritual. Agni serves as the mediator between humans and gods — offerings placed in the sacred fire are carried to the heavens. The verse highlights Agni's roles:

Purohita — The priest who stands before, leading the ceremony
Ritvij — The seasonal priest who maintains cosmic order
Hotar — The invoker who calls the gods to the sacrifice

Fire represents transformation, purification, and the spark of consciousness within all beings. It is through Agni that prayers ascend and blessings descend.

Hymn of Unity

Rig Veda 10.191.4 — A prayer for harmony and collective purpose

संगच्छध्वं संवदध्वं
संवो मनांसि जानताम् ।
देवा भागं यथा पूर्वे
संजानाना उपासते ॥
Transliteration:
Saṅgacchadhvaṃ saṃvadadhvaṃ
Saṃ vo manāṃsi jānatām
Devā bhāgaṃ yathā pūrve
Saṃjānānā upāsate

Word-by-Word:
सं-गच्छध्वम् (Saṃ-gacchadhvam) — come together, assemble (imperative plural)
सं-वदध्वम् (Saṃ-vadadhvam) — speak together, converse in harmony (imperative plural)
सम् (Sam) — together, in unity
वः (Vaḥ) — your (genitive plural)
मनांसि (Manāṃsi) — minds, hearts (nominative plural)
जानताम् (Jānatām) — may they know, let them understand (imperative)
देवाः (Devāḥ) — the gods (nominative plural)
भागम् (Bhāgam) — share, portion (accusative)
यथा (Yathā) — just as, in the way that
पूर्वे (Pūrve) — the ancients, those of old (nominative plural)
सं-जानानाः (Saṃ-jānānāḥ) — being of one mind, agreeing (nominative plural)
उपासते (Upāsate) — they worship, they sit together (3rd person plural)

Translation:
Come together, speak together,
Let your minds be in harmony.
Just as the ancient gods gathered together
In unity to receive their share of offerings.

Commentary

This beautiful hymn from the last book of the Rig Veda emphasizes the importance of unity, cooperation, and collective wisdom. It teaches that harmony among humans mirrors the harmony among the gods.

The verse is often chanted at the beginning of community gatherings, meetings, and collective endeavors. It reminds us that great achievements require unity of purpose, open communication, and alignment of hearts and minds.

Purusha Sukta

Rig Veda 10.90 — The Hymn of the Cosmic Person

सहस्रशीर्षा पुरुषः सहस्राक्षः सहस्रपात् ।
स भूमिं विश्वतो वृत्वात्यतिष्ठद्दशाङ्गुलम् ॥१॥

पुरुष एवेदं सर्वं यद्भूतं यच्च भव्यम् ।
उतामृतत्वस्येशानो यदन्नेनातिरोहति ॥२॥

एतावानस्य महिमातो ज्यायांश्च पूरुषः ।
पादोऽस्य विश्वा भूतानि त्रिपादस्यामृतं दिवि ॥३॥
Transliteration:
Sahasraśīrṣā puruṣaḥ sahasrākṣaḥ sahasrapāt
Sa bhūmiṃ viśvato vṛtvātyatiṣṭhaddaśāṅgulam (1)

Puruṣa evedaṃ sarvaṃ yadbhūtaṃ yacca bhavyam
Utāmṛtatvasyeśāno yadannenātirohati (2)

Etāvānasya mahimāto jyāyāṃśca pūruṣaḥ
Pādo'sya viśvā bhūtāni tripādasyāmṛtaṃ divi (3)

Word-by-Word (Verse 1):
सहस्रशीर्षा (Sahasra-śīrṣā) — thousand-headed
पुरुषः (Puruṣaḥ) — the Cosmic Person, the primordial being
सहस्राक्षः (Sahasrākṣaḥ) — thousand-eyed
सहस्रपात् (Sahasrapāt) — thousand-footed
सः (Saḥ) — he
भूमिम् (Bhūmim) — the earth (accusative)
विश्वतः (Viśvataḥ) — on all sides, everywhere
वृत्वा (Vṛtvā) — having covered, pervading
अत्यतिष्ठत् (Atyatiṣṭhat) — stood beyond, transcended
दशाङ्गुलम् (Daśāṅgulam) — by ten fingers' breadth

Word-by-Word (Verse 2):
पुरुष एव (Puruṣa eva) — the Purusha alone, indeed
इदम् सर्वम् (Idam sarvam) — all this (universe)
यत् भूतम् (Yat bhūtam) — whatever has been (past)
यत् च भव्यम् (Yat ca bhavyam) — and whatever will be (future)
उत (Uta) — and also
अमृतत्वस्य (Amṛtatvasya) — of immortality (genitive)
ईशानः (Īśānaḥ) — the lord, the ruler
यत् (Yat) — which
अन्नेन (Annena) — through food, through nourishment
अतिरोहति (Atirohati) — he transcends, he grows beyond

Word-by-Word (Verse 3):
एतावान् (Etāvān) — so much, this much
अस्य (Asya) — his
महिमा (Mahimā) — greatness, glory
अतः (Ataḥ) — than this
ज्यायान् (Jyāyān) — greater (comparative)
च (Ca) — and
पुरुषः (Puruṣaḥ) — the Cosmic Person
पादः (Pādaḥ) — a quarter, one-fourth
अस्य (Asya) — of him
विश्वा भूतानि (Viśvā bhūtāni) — all beings
त्रिपात् (Tripāt) — three-fourths
अस्य (Asya) — of him
अमृतम् (Amṛtam) — the immortal
दिवि (Divi) — in heaven

Translation:
The Cosmic Person has a thousand heads, a thousand eyes, a thousand feet.
He pervades the earth on all sides and extends beyond it by ten fingers' breadth. (1)

The Cosmic Person is all this — whatever has been and whatever shall be.
He is the Lord of immortality, which he outgrows through food. (2)

Such is his greatness, yet the Cosmic Person is greater still.
All beings are but a quarter of him; three-quarters are the immortal in heaven. (3)

Commentary

The Purusha Sukta is one of the most celebrated hymns in Vedic literature, describing the cosmic sacrifice from which the universe emerged. The "Purusha" (Cosmic Person) represents the primordial consciousness that pervades all existence.

Thousand heads, eyes, feet — Symbolizes the infinite, omnipresent nature of cosmic consciousness, present in all beings
Ten fingers beyond — The transcendent aspect that exceeds the material universe
Three-quarters immortal — Only one-fourth of the divine manifests as the visible universe; three-fourths remain unmanifest and eternal

This hymn profoundly influenced Hindu philosophy, establishing the concept that the universe is not separate from the divine but is a manifestation of cosmic consciousness. It teaches that all of creation — from celestial bodies to earthly beings — shares a common sacred origin.

Ganesha Suktam - Verse 9

Rig Veda 8.81.9 — Prayer for swift blessings (from the Ganapati Sukta)

स॒द्यो॒जुव॑स्ते॒ वाजा॑ अ॒स्मभ्यं॑ वि॒श्वश्च॑न्द्राः ।
वशै॑श्च म॒क्षू ज॑रन्ते ॥
Transliteration:
Sadyojuvas te vājā asmabhyaṃ viśvaścandrāḥ
Vaśaiś ca makṣū jarante

Word-by-Word:
सद्योजुवः (sadyo-juvaḥ) — swiftly moving, coming immediately
ते (te) — your
वाजाः (vājāḥ) — riches, strength, vigor
अस्मभ्यम् (asmabhyam) — for us, to us
विश्वश्चन्द्राः (viśva-ścandrāḥ) — all-delighting, pleasing to all
वशैः (vaśaiḥ) — with desires, with wishes
च (ca) — and
मक्षू (makṣū) — quickly, immediately
जरन्ते (jarante) — they sing praise, they invoke

Translation:
May your all-delighting riches come swiftly to us.
Filled with desires, people quickly sing your praises.

Commentary

This verse is the ninth mantra of the Ganesha Suktam, a compilation of Rig Vedic verses used in the worship of Lord Ganesha, the remover of obstacles. Though originally addressed to Indra in the Rig Veda, this verse has been adopted in Ganesha worship due to its powerful invocation for swift blessings.

Sadyojuva (Swift-moving) — Emphasizes the immediate nature of divine grace; Ganesha is known for quickly responding to sincere prayers
Vājā (Riches/Strength) — Not merely material wealth, but spiritual vigor, success in endeavors, and the removal of obstacles
Viśvaścandra (All-delighting) — The blessings bring joy to all, reflecting Ganesha's benevolent nature that benefits the entire universe

The Ganesha Suktam is traditionally chanted at the beginning of any important undertaking, ritual, or ceremony. This verse reminds devotees that when prayers are offered with sincere desire (vaśa), the Lord responds swiftly with blessings that delight all beings.
